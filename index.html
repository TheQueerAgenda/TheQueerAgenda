The Agenda

GERRRL, STOP THOSE SWEEPS

According to the January "Count Us In" report, 18% of the homeless population in Seattle were LBGTQI identified compared to 4.8% of Seattle’s population being LGBTQI. Between February 21 and April 30, the City spent $3 million on sweeps, which moved zero people into housing. We must listen to people experiencing homelessness and ground our policies in their voices. Let’s stop the ineffective sweeps and put our resources into what gets people indoors: long-term subsidies, stabilization while residing in encampments, taxing vacant housing, and tiny-house programs. If you agree, please sign up.

READ ME SOME QTBLGIA EDUCATION

What are LGBTQI youth taught in school? Our City's public school system is working towards incorporating ethnic studies into curriculum. Stories of LGBTQI experiences and those struggles are an imperative part of collective knowledge for all Seattle youth when they are coming of age. When LGBTQI youth are forming their identity, the absence of true, historical contributions from the LGBTQI community means less empowerment and lack of knowledge for young people aiming to learn. Lack of LGBTQI inclusion contributes to bullying and the overall health of LGBTQI youth. And curing bullying is through education, not punitive measures.

WHERE IS MY G’DAMN commUNITY SPACE!?

This conversation needs to move into a new direction. Rapid development is occurring all over Seattle and particularly in spaces that are concentrated by the LGBTQI community. But this development is not made to keep LGBTQI safe and healthy. It’s made for profit. Developments like buildings being built on top of the Capitol Hill light rail station have always had capacity to prioritize space for LGBTQI safety. Hate crimes are rising, seeing a 20% increase in occurrence in 2016 (article. Find the report, pdf, in the links on this page). And stickers on a storefront don’t make people safe. Prioritizing spaces for people to be free as themselves does. Our City also builds community centers Anderson d makes those spaces available to our neighborhoods. In Capitol Hill, the Miller Community Center sits ready to be transformed into a safe and health space for LGBTQI people. Community spaces must ensure safe space for people experiencing homelessness - a community space means a location where people can access safety, community, shelter, and services to address all of their needs. It’s time our City responds to the disproportionate amount of LGBTQI people experiencing homelessness and violence.

PRO-trans FOR LIIIFE

Is Seattle and is Washington as “progressive” as it touts itself? We champion free speech, but what about when that speech infringes upon the fundamental rights of one population in our community? Right now, Initiative 1552 is collecting signatures that would codify discrimination into Washington State law. This threat is real. And it cannot go unanswered. The City has demonstrated it’s progressive values on protecting marginalized communities through legal, social, and financial support. If any anti-trans policy is implemented in the State, our local government should rally behind the trans community in solidarity. We know this can be done through a legal defense fund in support of trans people like the City has already done to protect undocumented immigrants. It’s due time the City puts its money where its mouth is. YAAS!
